JENNA CASEY

i have always been one to really think and think and think when it comes to the question of “what do i want to do?” this process was absolutely no different for me. here i was thinking about what i wanted my last hoorah to be in this program. i still think about that now, but now everyone says they are so excited to see the end of my project, and i think that makes this whole thing even more intimidating. this all started from pulling together my interests, which i’ve really narrowed down in the past couple years. queerness, grief, memorialization, history, permanence, archiving. there’s so much there to work with, but i think at the root of everything, i like to spark emotion from the viewer. i want you to feel something deep, leaving different than you came in. at this point it almost feels as if it is expected of me to do something relating to these ideas now, well, really more in the history realm. what can i say, history is my thing: the portrayal of history in media, world conflict and the effects with that on countries, political upheaval, activism. i have become a little bit of the history nerd in this department… when i really spent time thinking about this project, i wasn’t even sure the idea i had was the idea i wanted. i found three images that felt inspirational to me and those centered on the ideas of queerness, world war ii, and archiving. from here i came up with the idea for my synthesis project, really as a means to combine all of these ideas together: archiving queer society during world war ii. naturally, i want to make my life difficult, right? well i picked a topic that hasn’t really been looked in to or documented. people say to me “when you first said this idea, my first thought was i didn’t know there were queer people during world war ii.” i think this really proves my point as a whole: the queer people were always there, they just had to hide that part of their identity because of how the world functioned at the time. it’s not anywhere near how society is now where there are so many queer people you will meet on a daily basis, and aspects of queer life are getting documented and archived. during world war ii this was not the case. so much is unknown. that’s what draws me to this project. i want to see what’s out there, i want to find the unknown. being someone who is queer, it’s very different eighty years later. The queer people in the 1940s didn’t get the chance to have a voice, and embrace their identities, while they were alive. i want to give them a platform to have that voice and really just give them back the voice that they lost. there is so much to learn from them. this project is full of questions to pose to the viewer. how do you access it? will it last forever? how do i handle the material? why? the list could go on and on. this project is one that plays off of the ideas expressed in history. how much should we know now that we couldn’t know then? should this be something fully accessible? something limiting? how can i make the viewer work to access the archive? how do i put constraints on the viewers interactions with the archive? taking something mostly physical and archiving that in a still physical way, not really changing its’ intended function. keeping it physical in a sense where it will not last forever; taking something that was never really documented and leaving it in a way where it is only partially accessible. playing with materiality to think about how it will degrade (paper) or fade (ink) over time. limiting the access to the viewer. something on a time limit. presenting it in a physical space, with a filing cabinet. half of the archive in the “files” within it. half of it not. that half, scattered on the ground around the filing cabinet. the lifespan of the pages in the hands that touch it. how do you access the files? how do you access the files? thinking... do you walk over the information on the ground? do you read what’s on the ground? do you look from a distance? how do you handle the materials, with both their actual materiality and with how it is presented? are you careful? do you preserve this history while it is still in front of you? how do you engage with the information knowing that it could be ruined at any moment, at the hands of those interacting with it, the history being wiped away from your grasp? so, again, i ask you: how do you access the files? all of this is for you to decide, but you have to work a little hard to get there... hehe good luck :)
